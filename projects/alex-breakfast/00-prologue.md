# Prologue: Before the Fall

![Prologue: Before the Fall](./prologue.png)

Four things are wrong with this Tuesday morning.

I'm eating cereal at the kitchen table, cataloging changes since yesterday:

1. Mom rearranged the spice rack. Alphabetical instead of by frequency of use. Suspicious.
2. Maya's phone is face-down on the counter. Usually glued to her hand. Very suspicious.
3. The mail came early. Mr. Patterson never delivers before 9 AM on Tuesdays. Extremely suspicious.
4. There's a new dent in the garage door. Small, fist-sized, about five feet up.

"You're doing it again." Maya doesn't look up from her phone. So much for the face-down theory.

"Doing what?"

"The detective thing. Squinty-eyes, scanning-the-room, Sherlock Holmes thing."

"I don't squint."

"You absolutely squint." She demonstrates, scrunching her face like she's reading fine print in a windstorm. "You go all..." Hands waving around her face. "*Analyzing*."

I squint at her. Just a little.

"Your phone's been on the counter for twelve minutes."

"Are you *timing* how long I'm not on my phone?"

"Face-down at 7:11. Picked up at 7:14. Put back down. Now 7:23." I shrug. "You're either avoiding someone, waiting for a specific message, or hiding something from me."

She stares like I've sprouted a second head. "You're so weird."

"I prefer 'observant.'"

"Weird." But she's smiling. "For your information, Mom asked me to actually have a conversation with you. Apparently I'm a 'distracted guardian' when she's at work."

"She's standing right there."

Mom turns around. Scrubs on. Hair in the practical bun that means twelve hours of chaos ahead. She gives me the Look. Half-amused, half-exasperated.

"I'm here *now*, Detective Finch. In twenty minutes, I won't be. Maya's in charge."

"Maya is on her phone."

"Maya is making an *effort*." Mom shoots my sister a meaningful glance.

Maya picks up her phone, unlocks it, sets it back down with exaggerated care. "See? Effort."

"What about the spice rack?"

Mom freezes mid-pour. "What about it?"

"You reorganized. Alphabetically. You never do alphabetical. You organize by frequency. Garlic, paprika, oregano, cumin, then the stuff we never touch. Now it's allspice, basil, cinnamon. Something's wrong."

Silence. Maya grins. Mom looks like she's deciding whether to laugh or ground me.

"Has anyone ever told you that you notice *too much*?"

"Frequently. But you're the one who taught me. 'Observation is a superpower, Alex. Most people sleepwalk through life.'"

Mom sighs. "I reorganized because I couldn't find the cinnamon and I was tired of digging. No mystery. No conspiracy. Just a tired nurse who wanted cinnamon in her coffee."

"And the garage door?"

Her smile falters. Just for a second.

"What dent?"

"Fist-sized. Five feet up. No dirt in the impact point. Happened within twelve hours."

Maya gets up to look. "Oh my god, there *is* a dent. Mom, did you hit something?"

"I... no." Mom sets down her mug, genuinely puzzled. "I didn't notice it yesterday."

"Storm last night," Maya offers. "Wind?"

"Wind doesn't punch circular dents." I stand to get a better look through the window. Clean damage. Deliberate. Not a branch. Something *hit* it.

"Baseball?"

"Too small. No ball in the yard."

Mom checks her watch. "I have to go. Maya's in charge. Alex, don't investigate the garage like it's a crime scene."

"It's not a crime scene. It's just anomalous."

"'Anomalous.'" Maya shakes her head. "You're thirteen. Say 'weird' like a normal person."

"I'm not normal. I'm a detective."

Maya catches my eye for a second. Something softer under the sarcasm. "Just don't go investigating anything dangerous, okay? I'm serious. Mom put me in charge, which means if you get hurt, it's *my* fault."

"I'm not going to get hurt looking at a garage door."

"I'm not talking about the garage door." She doesn't elaborate. Just that look, the one she gets sometimes when she thinks I'm not paying attention. The one that says she worries more than she admits.

Mom kisses the top of my head. "You're *my* detective. Grounded if I come home to yellow police tape around the garage."

"I don't have yellow police tape."

"Do *not* buy yellow police tape, Alex Finch."

Door closes. House settles. Maya scrolling. Me finishing cereal. Refrigerator humming off-key.

But my brain won't settle.

The dent. The spice rack. Early mail.

Probably nothing. Probably coincidence.

Detectives don't believe in coincidence.

We believe in patterns.
